Storage & Utilization Options in Carbon Capture and Storage Technology (CCT)

Once CO₂ is captured, the next crucial step is determining what to do with it. Carbon capture alone doesn’t reduce atmospheric emissions; the captured CO₂ must either be safely stored or utilized in valuable products. This article explores the main geological storage methods, key CO₂ utilization pathways, their technical feasibility, challenges, and potential for scale.

1. Geological Storage: The Backbone of CCT

Geological storage involves injecting compressed CO₂ deep underground where it can remain trapped for millennia. It is the primary long-term solution for permanent carbon storage.

Key Geological Storage Types

Depleted Oil and Gas Reservoirs

These are underground formations that have already held hydrocarbons for millions of years. They offer well-characterized geology and existing infrastructure, reducing risk and cost. Examples are Sleipner (Norway) and the Weyburn projects in Canada.

Deep Saline Aquifers

Porous rock formations saturated with brine far below freshwater aquifers. They represent the largest global CO₂ storage potential, but characterization is more complex than oil reservoirs. An example is the „Quest CCT“ in Canada.

Unmineable Coal Seams

These can adsorb CO₂ into coal pores, potentially increasing methane recovery (enhanced coalbed methane). Less common due to limited capacity and complex dynamics.

Storage Mechanisms

Once injected, CO₂ is trapped through multiple processes such as structural trapping, which is basically a physical barriers, like an impermeable caprock, which prevents upward migration. Another way to trap it is by trapping it residually, which just immobilizes CO₂ in small bubbles within pore spaces. Solubility trapping is also a way worth mentioning. CO₂ gets dissolved as soon as it gets in contact with formation water, which is the water that naturally is contained in oils or gases, and gets denser. The last method which is known to mankind is „mineral trapping“. Once CO₂ stays under the earth for a longer period of time it starts to react with minerals and forms stable carbonates.

Monitoring and Safety

Robust monitoring - using seismic surveys, pressure measurements, satellite data, and groundwater sampling - is mandatory to ensure containment and detect leaks early. Regulatory frameworks require long-term liability management.

2. Utilization: Turning CO₂ into Value

While storage is the dominant long-term pathway, Carbon Capture and Utilization (CCU) offers the possibility of turning CO₂ into commercial products, reducing net costs, and creating circular carbon economies.

Main Utilization Pathways

Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR)

Injecting CO₂ into oil fields to increase extraction efficiency. EOR can offset costs but is controversial because it produces fossil fuels; net emissions depend on system boundaries.

Building Materials

CO₂ can be mineralized into aggregates or used to cure concrete, improving strength and permanently locking carbon. Companies like CarbonCure and Solidia are commercializing such products. And there is the potential to sequester hundreds of millions of tonnes annually by 2050.

Synthetic Fuels and Chemicals

Captured CO₂ can be combined with renewable hydrogen to produce synthetic hydrocarbons, methanol, or polymers. This closes the carbon loop but requires large renewable energy input.

Food and Beverage Industry

CO₂ is used for carbonation, refrigeration, and packaging but this demand is comparatively small.

Limitations of Utilization

Scale: Current global CO₂ utilization is less than 1% of annual emissions; scaling is a challenge.
Durability: Many products eventually release CO₂ back to the atmosphere (e.g., fuels). Only mineralized CO₂ represents permanent removal.
Energy Intensity: Converting CO₂ to fuels or chemicals often requires large amounts of renewable energy to be carbon-neutral.

3. Infrastructure Considerations

Transport Networks: Pipelines are the most efficient for moving CO₂ from capture sites to storage or utilization hubs, especially for clusters of emitters. Ships, trucks, and rail are alternatives for smaller volumes or remote locations.

Storage Hubs and Clusters: Developing regional CO₂ hubs - where multiple emitters share pipeline and storage infrastructure - reduces capital costs and enhances scalability.

4. Future Prospects and Innovations

Direct Mineralization: Accelerating natural processes to convert CO₂ into stable minerals on the surface, enabling permanent storage without injection. Pilot projects underway globally.

Biological Utilization: Using algae and microbes to fix CO₂ into biomass or bio-products, potentially integrating with wastewater treatment.

Hybrid Storage: Combining CO₂ storage with hydrogen or geothermal projects to optimize subsurface use.

To sum it up, geological storage remains the most viable, scalable, and permanent solution for storing large volumes of captured CO₂, backed by decades of experience and advanced monitoring technologies. Utilization pathways, while promising, currently play a complementary role and can improve economics by generating revenue streams or products.

Together, storage and utilization form the foundation of CCT strategies that can achieve significant, durable reductions in atmospheric CO₂, helping to meet global climate goals.
