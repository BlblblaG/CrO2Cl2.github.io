The Chemistry of Carbon Capture and Storage Technology (CCT)

Carbon capture technology is grounded in chemical engineering and geochemistry. Understanding these fields helps explain how CO₂ is captured from industrial sources, purified, transported, and stored underground for centuries. This article explores the chemical principles and materials involved in each stage of CCT - from flue gas separation to underground mineralization.

1. CO₂ Capture: Core Chemical Mechanisms

The goal of the capture stage is to selectively separate CO₂ from a mixture of gases, which is usually flue gases from combustion, syngas from reformers or ambient air. This process is chemically complex, since CO₂ typecally comprises only 4–15% of the gas stream.

This article will explore the three main categories of chemical capture.

A. Post-Combustion Capture

This is the most widespread method, which involves chemically absorbing CO₂ after the fuel is burned. In Solvent Chemistry, the most common solvents are aqueous amines, especially monoethanolamine (MEA).

MEA reacts reversibly with CO₂ to form carbamate compounds:

R-NH₂ + CO₂ → R-NH-COO⁻ + H⁺

The CO₂-laden solvent is heated to ~120°C, which reverses the reaction and releases pure CO₂ for compression and storage. Newer Solvent Chemistry options include blended amines, amino acid salts and ionic liquids, which offer lower regeneration energy and greater stability.

B. Pre-Combustion Capture

In hydrogen and gasification systems, fossil fuels react with oxygen or steam to create syngas (CO + H₂).

The CO shifts to CO₂ through the water-gas shift reaction:

CO + H₂O → CO₂ + H₂

The CO₂ can then be removed using physical solvents, such as Selexol or Rectisol, at high pressure. These solvents are more effective when the CO₂ is highly concentrated.

C. Oxy-Fuel Combustion

Fuel is burned in pure oxygen instead of air. The resulting flue gas consists mainly of CO₂ and H₂O, which can be easily seperated by condensing the water vapor. Though the chemistry is simple, producing oxygen is energy intensive.

2. CO₂ Compression and Transport

Captured CO₂ is dehydrated and compressed to ~1.1-1.5 bar. It is then cooled into a supercritical fluid, where it behaves like both a liquid and a gas. This makes it dense enough for efficient pipeline transport.

In chemistry, the concern is that water must be fully removed to avoid the formation of carbonic acid:

CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃

This weak acid can corrode pipelines, so it’s essential to dry it.

3. Geological Storage Chemistry

Once injected into deep formations (800–3,000 meters), CO₂ undergoes four stages of geochemical interaction:

A. Structural Trapping

CO₂ is physically trapped beneath impermeable caprock. This happens immediately and is the most dominant mechanism early on.

B. Residual Trapping

Some CO₂ becomes immobilized as bubbles in pore spaces, held by surface tension - similar to water in a sponge.

C. Solubility Trapping

Over time, CO₂ dissolves into the saline formation water:

CO₂ (g) → CO₂ (aq) → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻

This reduces mobility and makes the fluid denser, causing it to sink - a stabilizing feedback loop.

D. Mineral Trapping

Over the course of decades or centuries, dissolved CO₂ reacts with formation minerals (e.g., Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) to form carbonates, such as calcite or magnesite:

Ca²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → CaCO₃

This is the most secure form of storage - essentially locking CO₂ into solid rock.

4. CO₂ Utilization Chemistry

Captured CO₂ can also be used as a feedstock in industrial processes:

Urea production:

2NH₃ + CO₂ → (NH₂)₂CO + H₂O

Concrete curing: CO₂ mineralizes in concrete, improving its strength and locking in carbon.

Synthetic fuels: Through reverse water-gas shift and Fischer-Tropsch synthesis, CO₂ + H₂ can be converted into hydrocarbons.

While these reactions are promising, they’re still costly and very energy-intensive.

In short, every step of CCT involves deep layer of chemistry, from gas absorption and solubility equilibria to phase behavior and geochemical mineralization. As new solvents, catalysts and materials are developed, the efficiency of CCT will continue to improve in effiency, making it not only technically, economically and environmentally feasible.
